between

five meditations on the productive space

Not the things themselves but what lives between them. The interval is not the notes. The gap is not absence.

agency

the gap

Viktor Frankl observed this in Auschwitz, which is to say he arrived at it in the place least designed to allow it: between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space is the power to choose a response. In the response lies growth and freedom. The observation has since been attributed to everyone — Covey, Aurelius, various motivational posters — and the attribution is now impossible to untangle. But it came, first, from a man who had almost every choice removed and who discovered that this one could not be taken.

It takes approximately 250 milliseconds for a sensory stimulus to reach conscious awareness. You are, at every moment, living slightly behind your own experience. Between the event and the experience of the event, something happens — the nervous system processes, filters, interprets. You call this immediate. It isn't. There is always a gap. The question is only whether it is used.

music

the interval

A musical interval is not a note. It is the relationship between notes — the distance, measured in frequency ratios, that defines whether two sounds form a fifth, a tritone, a major seventh. The interval does not exist in either note separately. It exists only between them, in the relationship, which is a third thing that is made of neither the first nor the second.

The same two notes played simultaneously create a chord. The same two notes played consecutively create a melody. The interval is mathematically identical in both cases. What changes is the dimension the distance exists in — harmonic or temporal, space or time. Music is organized relationships between sounds. Which means music is, at its foundation, made of betweens.

anatomy

the interstitium

Until 2018, the body contained a space without a name. Connective tissue throughout the body — beneath the skin, lining the digestive tract, surrounding the lungs — is laced with fluid-filled compartments, forming a pressurized network that had not been recognized as a distinct organ. The interstitium: the space between cells, an organ made entirely of in-between. It was hidden because the standard method of preparing tissue for examination collapses it. The fixative drains the fluid. What was a pressurized three-dimensional space becomes a thin wall. To see it, you have to look at living tissue. The act of fixing it destroyed what it was.

There is a methodological lesson buried here that applies more broadly: some things can only be observed in motion, in their living state. Fix them in place for examination and they become something else — the preparation produces the artifact, not the thing. The interstitium teaches this. It was always there. It only became visible when someone looked without fixing.

graph theory

betweenness

In network analysis, betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on the shortest path between other nodes. The node with the highest betweenness is not necessarily the one with the most connections. It is the node that bridges — the person through whom information must travel to get from one cluster to another. Remove them and the network fragments into groups that can no longer efficiently communicate. This person is usually not the most visible member of either group. They belong fully to neither. They live in the between, which is what makes them essential.

Social networks have betweenness nodes at every scale: the colleague who knows everyone in two departments, the friend who connects your old life to your new one, the person who is everyone's second phone call. They are not always the loudest. They are not always the ones with the most followers. They are the connective tissue — and like the interstitium, they become visible mostly when they're gone.

etymology

the threshold

The limen was the Roman threshold stone — the flat piece of stone at the entrance to a house, the boundary between inside and outside. It was sacred. Offerings were made to the spirit of the limen, the Limentinus. The threshold belonged to neither house nor street; it was the neither-here-nor-there, and therefore required its own deity. The modern word liminal inherits this sacredness: the liminal is not simply in-between, it is in a charged, productive, dangerous state of transition. Van Gennep identified three stages in every rite of passage — separation, liminality, incorporation — and the middle stage is the most important and the most uncomfortable.

We use threshold now for the minimum required to trigger a response: the pain threshold, the hearing threshold, the detection threshold. The word has moved from place to quantity. But the original meaning survives in the structure: a threshold is the boundary between states, the point at which one thing becomes another. The Romans built their thresholds out of stone and gave them a god. We use the word for the moment a signal becomes too loud to ignore. Both are the same idea: the between as a real and consequential location.
