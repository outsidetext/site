convergence

things arriving at the same point from different directions

eight paths · one destination · each at its own rate

The interesting question is not that they arrive together, but that they were always going to. The paths are different. The origins are different. The rates are different. The destination was not chosen by any of them individually. And yet.

instance I
Two people end up in the same city without knowing the other will be there. They moved for different reasons: one for work, one because a relationship ended, one because the rent was lower, one because someone they trusted said it was worth trying. They find each other eventually, or they don't. Either way, the city contained them both, already, before they knew it.
geographic
instance II
The exact moment when two separate long-running thoughts, developed independently over months, turn out to be the same thought seen from different sides. You are explaining one of them and the other arrives at the end of your own sentence. The convergence point was already there. You were approaching it from opposite directions without knowing the other path existed.
cognitive
instance III
Words from different etymologies, crossing different centuries, arriving at the same sound by different routes. Island and isle look related but aren't — island has an intrusive s borrowed from the unrelated isle through spelling influence alone. They converged in the mouth before they converged on the page. Language is full of these accidental meetings that look like family.
etymological
instance IV
Three separate conversations in the same week raise the same name, the same book, the same idea — from people who don't know each other and have no reason to be reading the same thing. You notice this. The question is whether the convergence is real or whether you are now primed to count mentions of a thing you would otherwise have filed away without registering. Both explanations involve you in the same position.
coincidental
instance V
Several independent evolutionary lineages developing the same solution to the same problem: the eye evolved separately in vertebrates and cephalopods; flight in birds and bats and insects; echolocation in bats and dolphins. The destination — a workable eye, an efficient wing — was fixed by physics and biology. Different routes, same arrival. Convergent evolution is the record of the destination being more real than the path.
evolutionary
instance VI
The end of a long friendship. It didn't end for one reason. Several things were moving independently for some time — a growing distance, a change in what either person needed, an incident that was smaller than what it revealed, the slow accumulation of decisions not to say something. They converged. Afterward, it is hard to say when it ended, because the ending was not a point. It was the meeting of several lines that had been traveling toward it for a while.
relational

Convergence is different from collision. In a collision, the paths end. In convergence, the paths arrive — and having arrived, they either hold at the point of meeting, or they cross through it and continue, now changed by what they passed through. The destination is real. What happens there depends on the angle of approach.
