proof

six attempts at formal demonstration

A mathematical proof establishes a statement beyond doubt through logical steps from accepted premises. The following theorems resist this treatment. The proofs are offered anyway — as a record of the attempt, and of where the argument ran out of road.

theorem 1
theorem
You will get over it.
Proof.

By induction. Base case: there exist things you have gotten over. You have evidence of this. There was something that felt permanent. It is no longer permanent. This constitutes a base case. Inductive step: assume you got over some prior thing of comparable magnitude; by continuity of the self, the current thing is also reachable. The induction step holds if the self is continuous, if prior cases are comparable, and if "getting over" is a stable category. Examine these assumptions. The self is not strictly continuous — it is revised at irregular intervals, sometimes substantially. Prior cases may not be comparable; each event has a specific relationship to a specific version of you, and that version may not persist. "Getting over" may describe a threshold experience or a gradual one or an asymptotic approach that is technically never completed but passes some practical threshold. Assuming these complications are resolvable and not definitionally fatal to the proof: the theorem is likely true, in a practical sense, for most definitions of the terms involved. The proof cannot be completed to mathematical standard because the terms are not formally defined. Work continues.

theorem 2
theorem
The moment was as good as you remember it.
Proof.

Attempted by direct comparison. To compare the memory to the moment, we require access to the moment. The moment is not currently accessible. It existed at a specific point in time that has passed. The memory is a reconstruction, not a recording — each retrieval modifies the stored version slightly, and the modifications accumulate. What we have is a sequence of reconstructions. The original is unavailable for inspection. Further: even at the time, the moment as experienced was filtered through attention, mood, and prior expectation, none of which were calibrated for maximum accuracy. It may have been better than memory suggests — memory tends to flatten affect. It may have been worse — memory tends toward the dramatic. The theorem cannot be established or disproved. We note that the impossibility of proof does not affect the quality of the memory as an experience. The memory continues to function regardless of its accuracy. This may be the relevant property.

∎
theorem 3
theorem
It was the right decision.
Proof.

By exhaustion of alternatives. To prove a decision was right, we show that all alternatives were worse. We require access to the outcomes of alternatives not taken. The counterfactual outcomes are not available. They can be estimated, but every estimate is produced by the same reasoning apparatus that made the original decision, which introduces systematic bias toward confirming that decision. Alternative: we show the decision was correct by the information available at the time. This is more tractable. What was known at the time of decision? What were the stated reasons? Were those reasons coherent? Were they the actual reasons or post-hoc rationalizations? This investigation may not converge on a satisfying result. Note that the question itself may be malformed. "Right" requires a metric; the choice of metric is not neutral; different metrics yield different verdicts. The search for the right metric is itself a kind of decision, subject to the same problem. Proof deferred pending metric selection.

theorem 4
theorem
That person knew you.
Proof.

Left as an exercise to the reader. Note: the exercise is more difficult than it appears. "Knew" is not a binary property. Knowing someone operates at multiple depths — behavioral prediction, emotional pattern recognition, access to the history of the self — and a person may know you in some registers and entirely miss others. They may know you accurately in the past tense and not in the present. They may know a version of you that you have since revised. The felt sense of being known is not always reliable evidence of being known; nor is its absence reliable evidence of the opposite. Some people know without showing it. Some show knowing without having it. Further complication: you may not have complete access to whether you are known, as this would require knowing yourself to the same depth, which is not guaranteed. The exercise is provided without a solution key. You have been working on it for some time.

theorem 5
theorem
Something good will happen.
Proof.

By appeal to probability. Consider the sample space of all possible future events. "Good" events exist within this space — they have occurred in the past, establishing that they are possible. Assuming the distribution of events is not strictly declining toward zero probability of good outcomes — an assumption that is not always easy to hold but is also not well-supported by the evidence as false — some good event will occur. The proof holds for sufficiently broad definitions of "good." For narrow definitions, the probability is harder to establish. The question is usually not whether something good will happen — this is nearly certain — but whether the specific good that is wanted will happen, for which no probability can be named. The theorem, carefully interpreted, is true and not comforting. The version people are usually looking for requires a different proof technique, which does not exist.

∎
theorem 6
theorem
This is enough.
Proof.

Insufficient evidence. The theorem may be true. It cannot be shown to be true by examining the evidence, because the assessment of sufficiency uses the same instrument whose calibration is in question. If you feel that this is not enough, that feeling is produced by the same apparatus that would need to confirm the theorem. If you feel that it is enough, that feeling is equally suspect. The correct response is not to abandon the question but to note that the question has a property that pure mathematics does not: it answers itself in the practice of living rather than in the proof. You are already inside the demonstration. Whether the theorem holds is not separable from what you do next. The proof completes when something else happens, which will happen or not, and will be enough or not, for reasons the theorem cannot reach. We note that a great deal depends on this one and leave it without a symbol.
