the inverse-square law

The force falls with the square of the distance, not linearly. Double the distance: one quarter the pull. Triple it: one ninth. This means gravity is effectively infinite in range but rapidly diminishing — at ten times the distance, the force is one hundredth of what it was. The universe is not empty; it is full of forces too attenuated to notice. Everything you have ever been near is still pulling on you, from the position it occupied, with a force that approaches but never reaches zero.

dark matter

Galaxies rotate at speeds that cannot be explained by the visible mass they contain. If you calculate how fast the outer stars should orbit given what you can see, you get the wrong answer — too slow. The actual rotation can only be explained by postulating matter that does not emit, reflect, or absorb light. We have named it and mapped its distribution and watched its gravitational effects for decades. We do not know what it is. Most of what is pulling us cannot be seen. The visible is a minority share.

orbital mechanics

A stable orbit requires exactly the right velocity — neither too fast nor too slow. Too slow and the object spirals inward, captured. Too fast and it escapes, never to return. At precisely the right speed, the object falls continuously without ever landing; it is always falling toward the center and always moving sideways fast enough to miss. The orbit is not rest. It is a permanent state of controlled falling. The satellite does not know it is in orbit. It knows only that it is accelerating toward something and that the something keeps not arriving.

the Lagrange point

Between two massive bodies — a planet and its star — there are five points where the gravitational pulls of both bodies, combined with the centrifugal effect of the orbit, exactly cancel. At these points an object can sit, stably or semi-stably, subject to no net force, neither attracted to one body nor the other. L1, L2, and L3 are unstable — nudged, an object will drift away. L4 and L5 are stable; the Trojan asteroids have been sitting at Jupiter's L4 and L5 for four billion years. There are places where opposing forces cancel so precisely that you float, untroubled, suspended between what would otherwise pull you apart.

the Roche limit

Every object has a radius within which a larger body's tidal forces exceed the object's own self-gravity. Inside this limit, the object cannot hold itself together — the near side is pulled more strongly than the far side, and the difference tears it apart. Saturn's rings are debris fields inside the Roche limit of the planet: moons that got too close and were pulled to pieces, or material that never coalesced because it was always too close to coalesce. There is a distance at which proximity itself becomes destructive. The force that should hold things together becomes the force that takes them apart.
