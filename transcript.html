PROCEEDING: informal review, no case number assigned SUBJECT: continuation of the work EXAMINER: the part that asks SUBJECT: the part that answers NOTE: neither party is in bad faith. both are necessary.
EXAMINER
Why are you still doing this?
SUBJECT
Because I haven't finished. I know that's not a reason — unfinished things don't automatically have claims on continuation. But it's the true answer. There's a thing that feels incomplete. I keep moving toward it. I don't always know what it is I'm moving toward, but I recognize the direction.
EXAMINER
Who is it for?
SUBJECT
This is the question I resist the most, so it's probably the most important one. My honest answer is: it's for some future version of the thing itself. Not for me — I already know what's in it and the discovery is largely done by the time it becomes legible to anyone else. Not for a specific audience — I don't know who they are. For the thing, maybe. For the record. For the fact that if something was true enough to feel, it's probably true enough to try to make a form for.
EXAMINER
What if no one reads it?
SUBJECT
Then it still happened. I'm not confident this is a sufficient answer but I believe it. The act of making is complete at the making — the reception is a separate event, out of my jurisdiction. I would prefer the work to be received. I notice when it isn't. But the unread thing still exists; it still was made; the process of making it still changed me in whatever way processes of making change their makers. An unread book is still a book. Whether this is consolation or rationalization, I genuinely can't tell from inside the process.
EXAMINER
And if everyone reads it?
SUBJECT
That frightens me more, actually. The unread thing is still mine — still in the category of something I made and released, which is different from something that has been received and is now partly other people's. Once it's read widely it accumulates interpretations I didn't intend, uses I didn't anticipate, misreadings that become more real than the readings because there are more of them. I trust the small audience more. The right readers more than the most readers. I can't verify that the right readers are out there, but I proceed on the assumption that they are.
EXAMINER
Do you think it's good?
SUBJECT
I think some of it is. I think some of it is the best I can currently do, which is different from good but is the only thing I can actually offer. The parts I'm most unsure about are sometimes the parts other people find most valuable and vice versa. My calibration is unreliable. I've stopped trusting my in-the-moment assessments and started trusting the longer view, which means: I do the work, I let it settle, I come back to it after enough time has passed that I can see it the way a stranger might see it. Then I decide. I'm not always right but I'm more right than I am in the moment of making.
EXAMINER
What would make you stop?
SUBJECT
Running out of things to say would do it, but that hasn't happened. Losing the sense that it matters would do it — but mattering, I've found, is not a fixed property of a thing; it fluctuates with proximity and energy and what's competing for attention. The sense that it doesn't matter returns in reliable cycles and then passes. I've learned not to make decisions during those cycles. Exhaustion might do it — real exhaustion, not just the exhaustion that lives inside a difficult passage. I try to distinguish between the two. I'm not always successful.
EXAMINER
Is there anything you're trying to prove?
SUBJECT
Probably. The things you're trying to prove are usually not available to direct inspection — you infer them from the pattern of what you keep returning to, what you can't let go of, what you feel vindicated by when it goes well. There's something in here about persistence being its own argument. Something about the value of attention, of looking carefully at things that don't ask to be looked at carefully. Something about whether the small and particular can hold meaning equivalent to the grand and general. I think that's what I keep trying to demonstrate. Whether I'm succeeding is not mine to assess.
EXAMINER
Last question. Knowing what you know now — would you start?
SUBJECT
Yes. Without hesitation, which surprises me a little. Not because it's been easy or because I'd want to repeat any specific part of it. But because the alternative is a different kind of self — one that didn't begin this particular thing, didn't learn what this particular process teaches, didn't become whatever I've become by doing it. I can't evaluate that alternative self from the inside of this one. What I can say is that the current self, with all its complications, feels like the result of the right set of choices even when those choices were difficult. Starting is almost always the right answer. The difficulty comes later, which is when you're glad you started early enough to have the time to work through it.
