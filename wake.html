hydrodynamics

the Kelvin angle

A ship's wake forms a V at 19.47 degrees regardless of speed. Lord Kelvin proved this in 1887: the angle is not a function of velocity but of gravity wave dispersion — the relationship between wavelength and propagation speed in water under gravity. Faster ships don't widen the wake. They concentrate more energy into the same angle, making the pattern more pronounced but not broader. The invariant angle is a fact about water, not about the ship.

In 2013, physicists showed that the angle does narrow at very high Froude numbers — the ratio of a vessel's speed to the speed of shallow-water waves — revising the century-old result at the extremes. But for the vast middle range of human seafaring, Kelvin's result holds: every vessel, from a kayak to a supertanker, leaves the same angle of disturbance. The wake is a geometric consequence. The ship is almost incidental to it.

consciousness

hypnagogia

The state between sleeping and waking has a name: hypnagogia. Alpha waves fragment into theta; geometric hallucinations appear — grids, tunnels, faces — and voices speak from no source. The hypnic jerk, the sudden muscle spasm at sleep onset, may be the brain correcting for an imagined fall: a ghost-reflex for a drop that wasn't happening, triggered by the nervous system interpreting its own transition as a threat. The threshold produces its own phenomena, distinct from what lies on either side.

Edison and Dalí allegedly slept in chairs holding steel balls over metal plates, to be woken by the clatter at the moment of sleep onset and harvest the hypnagogic images before they dissolved. The claim is that the most generative zone is the transition, not the destination. Neither waking nor sleeping, the threshold state produces what neither state alone can generate. The wake is the productive part. The sleep is where you're going.

mourning

the vigil

The Irish wake keeps the body in the house for one to three days, surrounded by people eating, drinking, telling stories about the dead. The word itself: Old English wacu, to stay awake watching over something. The function is metabolizing a fact the body cannot immediately absorb. Death is a large piece of information. It arrives faster than comprehension. The rituals that follow are the comprehension — slow, social, sustained.

Jewish shiva lasts seven days. Muslim mourning extends forty days. Buddhist ceremonies can span weeks. Each formalizes what grief requires: time inside the current of ordinary social life, the acute grief housed and shaped rather than suppressed. Without shape, grief disperses without dissipating. The vigil is the structure that holds the dissolution long enough for something to change. The wake is the organized interval between the fact and the fact being integrated.

aviation

wake turbulence

Large aircraft generate counter-rotating wingtip vortices as a consequence of producing lift. These vortices sink behind the aircraft, spread laterally, and persist for up to three minutes in calm air. A small aircraft encountering a heavy jet's wake can lose control — the vortices are powerful enough to exceed the roll authority of a light plane. Controllers enforce separation minima of four nautical miles behind a heavy aircraft at the same altitude. The hazard is invisible and detectable only by its effects: sudden rolling, unexpected sink rate.

The aircraft behind flies in the organized consequence of the aircraft ahead's passage through the medium — experiencing the disturbance whether or not it was invited. The sky is not empty after the aircraft passes. It holds the shape of the passage for minutes. The smaller aircraft doesn't encounter the airliner. It encounters what the airliner left in the air: a wake, a rotating violence, the physics of having been there.

digital

the cached ghost

When a website goes dark, it doesn't disappear. Wayback Machine snapshots, search engine caches, hot-linked images on other sites, block quotes in forum threads — the site's wake persists in the distributed medium of the web for years after the domain lapses. Pages that no longer exist are still quoted, linked, described. The description outlasts the thing described. The citation survives the cited.

What haunts the web is structure more than content. The shape of a site's linkage can be partially reconstructed even when the pages are gone: who linked to it, what they said about it, what it linked to in turn. A link graph is the record of a presence. The 404 is not the end of the thing. It is the point at which retrieval becomes a project instead of a click — the moment the wake becomes the only evidence that the ship was ever there.
