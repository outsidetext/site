portraits

small moments, described as exactly as possible.

i. the signal

On a train through a valley, she watched the signal bar drop from two to one to nothing. For eleven minutes she had no connection to anything outside the window. She felt, briefly, the specific lightness of being unreachable. When the bar returned, she did not check her phone immediately. She gave herself another thirty seconds. This is the most privacy she gets most days.

ii. the draft

At 2:17am she wrote a message she had been composing in her head for six weeks. She read it back. It was accurate. She held her finger over send for a moment, then put the phone face-down on the mattress. At 8:52am she deleted it. At 11:03am she sent a shorter version that said less and meant the same thing. The first version was the true one. The one that got sent was the one she could live with having sent.

iii. the building

A man watched from the same third-floor window every morning as a building across the street came down. This took four weeks. Each morning there was less of it. He grew to organize his coffee around the progress — a ritual he hadn't chosen but had come to depend on. On the last morning there was nothing left to watch, and he stood at the window for a moment longer than necessary anyway, cup in hand, looking at the gap where it had been.

iv. the library book

Inside a returned library book — pressed between pages 214 and 215 — was a handwritten note on the back of a grocery receipt. It had not been meant to be left there. The librarian read it, understood immediately that it was private, and placed it in the recycling bin without telling anyone. She thought about it intermittently for three years. She never found out who had written it or what happened next.

v. the last dinner

They had dinner at the same restaurant on the first Thursday of every month for nine years. The waitstaff knew their order. On the last night, neither of them knew it was the last night. They argued briefly about something that didn't matter, paid, and walked out separately to save time. This is how most last nights go: indistinguishable from any other night until later, in retrospect, when the absence of ceremony becomes its own kind of ceremony.

vi. the name

Somewhere right now, someone is thinking your name. Not your full name — your first name, the one people use when they want to get your attention. They know a version of you that you may have forgotten: something you said once, how you looked in a particular light, a small habit you've since stopped. You have completely forgotten this person. They have not completely forgotten you. This is not unusual. It happens constantly, in both directions, to everyone.

vii. the tab

He opened a browser tab in November with the intention of reading the article later that day. The article was about something he had been meaning to understand for years — a real gap in his knowledge that he had decided, that morning, to finally close. In March the laptop died. He never finished the article. He still thinks about it sometimes. Not about what it said, but about the version of himself who had opened it so deliberately, so full of intention, on a Tuesday in November.

viii. the translation

She translated a sentence from one language into another. In the original, it meant longing for a place you've never been. In her translation, it came out as homesickness for the future. Both were technically wrong. Both were closer to true than anything she could have said directly. She kept the translation and deleted the original. Some losses are worth it.

ix. the photograph

In a photograph taken at a party in 2011, there are fourteen people in the frame. Eleven of them are looking at the camera. Two are looking at each other. One is looking slightly off to the left at something outside the frame — something no one else noticed, something the photograph can never show. Everyone who was at the party has seen this photograph. Nobody has ever mentioned the fourteenth person's expression. It is, in retrospect, the most interesting thing in the image.

x. the receipt

She found a receipt in the pocket of a coat she hadn't worn in two years. It was from a grocery store, dated a Tuesday in March: milk, bread, the expensive pasta, two lemons, something that had been marked down. She had no memory of this particular Tuesday. But she had clearly been there, buying food, living a life whose details she could no longer access. The receipt was evidence of a day that had meant nothing at the time and would never mean anything now. She put it back in the pocket.
