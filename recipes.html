How to Prepare Nostalgia
serves: 1 · cannot be shared, though often attempted
ingredients
  • one photograph you can't quite reach
  • a song you haven't heard in several years
  • a particular quality of afternoon light (the golden hour, specifically, around 4pm in autumn)
  • one object that belonged to a different version of you
  • time, unsupervised
method

Begin with something small — a smell, or a sound. Do not seek it out; let it find you. When it arrives, do not try to hold it still. Nostalgia cannot be pinned. It only passes through. The feeling will come when it is ready, usually at a quiet moment when you weren't watching for it. Sit with it. Let it move through the room like weather.

Nostalgia tastes slightly different every time. This is because it is not memory. It is the feeling of almost-remembering.
A Recipe for Waiting Well
serves: all durations equally
ingredients
  • one unanswered message, recent
  • a window (any size, any view)
  • something to do with your hands
  • the decision not to check again for at least ten minutes
method

Arrange yourself near the window. Set no timer — timers make waiting hostile. Give your hands something gentle: a cup, a pen, a page. Permit yourself to notice what is outside the window. You are not waiting. You are simply here, and something will happen later. The distinction matters.

Best served without a phone nearby. Substituting phone-checking for patience does not produce the same result.
How to Make Silence
serves: the whole room
ingredients
  • absence of speech (at least ninety seconds)
  • two or more people who trust each other
  • something worth listening to beneath the quiet
method

Stop. All at once if possible. Do not fill the pause with filler words or nervous laughter. Let the other sounds come in: the building, the street, the season. Silence between people who know each other is not empty. It is a different kind of full.

Shared silence that feels comfortable is a sign of something. Pay attention to who you can be quiet with.
The Recipe for Leaving Somewhere
serves: 1 · yields enough grief to last the drive home
ingredients
  • a place you have been long enough to belong to
  • one last walk through it, alone
  • the knowledge that you will not return in the same way
  • something small to take with you — not a souvenir, a detail: the way the light looked at 7am, the specific sound of a particular door
method

Do the last walk slowly. Look at ordinary things. The window you looked out of every morning. The floor's particular creak. Leaving is not only the moment of departure — it begins when you know you're leaving. Let it begin early. Let it run its full course. You cannot rush it without losing something.

The grief of leaving is proportional to how much you were actually there. This is not a bad thing.
How to Prepare Wonder
serves: any number · increases with sharing
ingredients
  • one fact about something you thought you understood
  • a question you cannot answer yet
  • the willingness to feel small without feeling diminished
method

Read about something you never thought to read about — a biological process, a geological event, the lifespan of a particular star. Let it be inconsequential to your daily life. That is the point. Inconsequential wonder is the purest kind. The feeling of suddenly understanding how large everything is, without being frightened by it, is wonder at its best.

Wonder does not require drama. It works equally well with ant colonies, prime numbers, and the fact that octopuses have three hearts.
A Recipe for Making Peace with a Decision
serves: 1 · the other options are not invited
ingredients
  • the decision (already made)
  • a full accounting of what you knew at the time
  • the understanding that the self who decided was doing their best
  • a willingness to stop re-running the alternatives
method

The hardest ingredient to find is the last one. Alternatives are compelling because they are always theoretical — untested by reality, perfect in imagination. The decision you made has already been tested. It has a shape. The others are only shadows of shapes. Prefer the real, even when it is harder.

This recipe takes longer than most. Some batches take years. Start anyway.
How to Make Sunday Last
serves: the whole day · pairs well with no plans
ingredients
  • no obligations before noon
  • something to read that isn't work
  • one meal cooked slowly, without a recipe
  • a conversation that goes nowhere useful
  • the decision not to prepare for Monday until Monday
method

The most common mistake is trying to be productive on Sunday. Sunday is not a small Monday. It is its own thing. Resist the urge to optimize it. Read slowly. Cook something warm. Talk to someone about nothing in particular. The day lengthens when it is not being used for anything.

Sunday lasts longer when you are not watching the clock for Monday's approach. The clock knows. You don't have to help it.
The Recipe for Arriving Somewhere New
serves: 1 in unfamiliar territory
ingredients
  • a place you don't know yet
  • no agenda for the first hour
  • the willingness to get briefly lost
  • a question you would only ask a stranger
method

Before you consult the map, walk. Any direction. New places reveal themselves through detours and wrong turns. The thing you will remember best from any trip is rarely on the itinerary. It is the coffee shop you found by accident. The street that looked interesting. The alley with the good light. Give the first hour to chance.

Familiarity arrives faster than expected. On the third day you will know which direction is north. On the seventh you will have a favourite route. Savour the disorientation while it lasts.
