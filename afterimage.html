how it works

When you stare at a color, the photoreceptors in your eyes that respond to that wavelength become fatigued. When you look away, those cells are still recovering — so the area your eyes project the image onto appears to glow in the complementary color. The amber-orange becomes blue-green. The bright becomes dim.

This is called a negative afterimage. It happens without permission. Every color you've looked at for long enough has left its ghost on your retina before clearing. What you see when you close your eyes is, in part, the accumulated residue of what you've been looking at.

The word you were staring at was HERE. The word in the afterimage is the same word, inverted. The content doesn't change. Only the color of how it reaches you.

Memory works similarly. What you recall is not the event but a reconstruction assembled from whatever receptors weren't fatigued at the time. The afterimage of a thing is always present. It just requires the right background to see it.
