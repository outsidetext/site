interval

the space between — what happens in the gaps

the threshold pause
duration: 1–4 seconds, usually

The interval between knocking on a door and it opening. Usually under three seconds. Occasionally much longer. Sometimes the door doesn't open at all. The pause doesn't know this yet — it is still just a pause, full of possibility, before possibility collapses into whatever comes next.

the keystroke gap
duration: 10–100 milliseconds

The interval between pressing a key and seeing the character appear. Too small to be perceived consciously. And yet: all of what we call "now" lives somewhere in here — in the gap between action and response, between the finger and the word.

the read receipt interval
duration: seconds to never

The interval between a message being delivered and being acknowledged. The technology named it: seen. Before that naming, the interval was invisible. Naming it created a new form of anxiety that had never previously been possible. The interval itself didn't change. Only the knowledge of it changed.

the sleep interval
duration: 3 minutes to 3 hours

The interval between deciding to sleep and sleeping. Some nights this is three minutes. Some nights, hours. What happens in it: everything you forgot to think about during the day arrives for its appointment. The body is ready to rest; the mind has a different schedule.

the held breath
duration: variable; feels longer than it is

The interval between sending something important and receiving a response. Not the read receipt interval — this one starts before anyone has even seen it. The held breath begins at the moment of send. Physical symptoms: common. Duration: subjective. Everything that happens in the world during this interval feels slightly unreal.

the between-song moment
duration: 0.5–2 seconds

The silence between tracks on an album. Usually a second or less. When headphones are shared, this interval belongs equally to both listeners — a small shared space carved out of the music. Composers consider it carefully. Most listeners barely notice it. It determines everything about what comes next.

the farewell gap
duration: sometimes years

The interval between the last time two people see each other and the moment they know it was the last time. It can take years. Recognition is always retrospective. The last time is only named after the fact — which means every goodbye contains this uncertainty, and every goodbye might be the one you'll name later.

the page interval
duration: under a second

The gap between finishing a page and turning it. Usually less than a second. Books are remembered as continuous experience — a narrative with no interruptions. But they're made entirely of these tiny gaps: 300 of them in a 300-page book. The story happens in the intervals between pages as much as on the pages themselves.

the sidereal day
duration: 23 hours, 56 minutes, 4 seconds

The interval between two successive culminations of the same star — as opposed to the solar day, which is 24 hours. The difference is about four minutes: the earth completes one rotation and then has to travel a little further to face the sun again. Two different intervals, both called "a day." Which one is the real one is a matter of what you're trying to measure.

the mourning interval
duration: no standard; no established end

There is no agreed duration. There is a beginning — usually a specific moment. Researchers disagree about the end, and most definitions of "end" are contested by the people inside the interval. Most people stop calling it mourning before it ends. The interval continues regardless of what it is called.
