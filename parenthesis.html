parenthesis

the material that was almost cut

What follows is a collection of parenthetical asides. The sentences they belonged to have been removed. The main text that gave them context is gone. What remains is the bracketed part — the qualification, the admission, the thing said in passing on the way to the thing that was said. Click possible context to see what might have surrounded it.

aside · I
(which came as a surprise, given how certain everyone had been beforehand)

The parenthetical is the honest part. The main clause says what we meant to say. The parenthetical says what we noticed on the way. It is structurally marked as secondary — smaller, enclosed, optionally skippable — but it often contains the admission that makes the rest true. The aside is where certainty goes to be qualified. The parenthesis is where you put what you know but cannot quite lead with.

possible context
The project succeeded (which came as a surprise, given how certain everyone had been beforehand), and the team was invited to present its results at the following year's conference.
aside · II
(or something like it — something in that direction, at least, for a moment)

The parenthetical hedge is not cowardice. It is precision attempting to survive into language. The experience was real; the word for it is approximate; the parenthesis acknowledges the gap between the two. Something like joy. Something in the direction of understanding. Something that felt, for a moment, like arrival. The bracket is where honesty about the limits of available vocabulary goes, so the main clause can still proceed without lying.

possible context
She said she was happy (or something like it — something in that direction, at least, for a moment), and she meant it as accurately as that kind of statement can be meant.
aside · III
(though it had always been possible, which everyone would later agree had been obvious)

Hindsight reorganizes the sequence. In the past, the outcome was uncertain. In the future, looking back, it was always inevitable. The parenthesis that says which everyone would later agree is making a temporal correction inside a sentence — it is performing an act of time travel, embedding the future's perspective into the present account. Most things look inevitable in hindsight. The parenthesis is where you put the knowledge that this is a distortion, without being able to fully prevent the distortion from occurring.

possible context
The wall came down in 1989 (though it had always been possible, which everyone would later agree had been obvious) with a suddenness that caught the intelligence services of multiple nations by surprise.
aside · IV
(among other things, and not necessarily in this order, and the order may not be recoverable)

The list is one of the oldest organizing structures in writing. It implies sequence, priority, completeness. The parenthetical that follows a list and says not necessarily in this order is a retraction of what the list implied — an admission that the form has misrepresented the content. Lists sort. Reality doesn't always sort. The parenthesis is where the writer acknowledges the cost of using the structure: clarity at the expense of accuracy, an ordering imposed on material that did not arrive ordered and may not have been ordered at all.

possible context
The things she remembered from that year: the cold January, the conversation at the kitchen table, the morning she decided (among other things, and not necessarily in this order, and the order may not be recoverable) to stop waiting for the version of events that would make everything make sense.
aside · V
(this being the exception rather than the rule, which we note here and then proceed to generalize from anyway)

The parenthetical disclaimer is the form's most self-aware gesture. Here the writer knows the evidence is insufficient, acknowledges this within the text, and then continues to draw the conclusion the evidence technically does not support. The parenthesis performs the methodological conscience while the main clause performs the intellectual convenience. This is not dishonesty. It is the ordinary condition of writing about anything: the available evidence never fully supports the necessary claim, and the parenthesis is where you put the gap, so that the claim can be made at all.

possible context
One city had managed the transition without significant conflict (this being the exception rather than the rule, which we note here and then proceed to generalize from anyway), suggesting that with the right conditions in place, peaceful reorganization was achievable.
aside · VI · unclosed
(and here is where it gets complicated, or rather, here is where it was always complicated and it simply becomes visible, which is not the same as the complication beginning — the complication began long before this point, in a place and at a time that neither party would agree on, since the beginning of a complication is itself subject to interpretation, and the interpretation tends to favor whoever began keeping records first

Some parentheses are not closed. The formal explanation is that the writer forgot. The charitable explanation is that the material inside could not be fully enclosed — that the aside became the thing itself, that the qualification swallowed the sentence it was qualifying, that what was put in brackets turned out to be the story, and the main clause it was supposed to support was the thing that was never quite true. The unclosed parenthesis is the honest form for certain kinds of content. It says: I began to qualify this and found I could not stop. The bracket opened. It remains open. Everything after this point is inside it.
