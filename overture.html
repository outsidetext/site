    architecture

    the function of the opening

    An overture is not the piece. It introduces themes that will be developed, characters who will return, motifs that will arrive in fuller form later. It creates expectation and then defers it. The purpose is not to summarize but to prime: to arrange the listener's attention in the posture required to receive what follows. The overture ends, and then the actual thing begins. What the overture has done is make you ready for it in a way you weren't before the first notes.

    Not every piece of music has a formal overture. But every piece of music has something that does overture's work: the silence before the first note, the quality of the opening, the establishing of key and tempo and mode. Even a song that starts on the first beat without introduction is making a choice about what kind of beginning this is. The overture can be very short. Its absence is also a statement.

    music

    leitmotif

    Wagner developed the technique of associating a brief musical theme with a character, place, object, or idea — and then repeating, transforming, and combining these themes throughout a work. The Ring Cycle has over a hundred leitmotifs, which the attentive listener unconsciously learns to recognize over the course of sixteen hours of music spread across four operas. The overture introduces them before you know what they mean. Later, when the sword theme returns in a minor key, you feel its weight through the accumulated memory of having heard it before under different circumstances.

    This is what the overture teaches: the themes are planted before you know they are seeds. The repeated motif accumulates meaning with each recurrence. By the end of the work, a phrase that was weightless in the overture is carrying everything the work has built. You can't know this on first hearing. You only understand the beginning fully after you've reached the end — which is one reason the overture is the last thing some composers wrote.

    writing

    the first sentence as contract

    A first sentence is a promise about what kind of reading experience this will be. It establishes register, pace, the relationship between the reader and the material. "Call me Ishmael" says: this will be direct, and strange, and will ask you to hold both lightness and enormity simultaneously. "It is a truth universally acknowledged" says: this will be precise, ironic, and will expect you to follow the gap between what is asserted and what is meant. The reader has been told what to prepare for, in a language that only becomes legible in retrospect.

    The first sentence cannot be half-right. It can only be right for this work or wrong. Everything that follows must be consistent with what the opening promised — not in content but in kind, in the quality of attention it asks for. A first sentence that promises careful observation cannot be followed by pages of carelessness. A first sentence that is funny cannot precede a book that doesn't know what to do with humor. The overture commits you. The rest of the work must make good on it.

    composition

    written last

    The overture is often written last. Composers knew they needed to begin the performance with something, so they returned once the main work was finished and wrote an introduction to what they had made. The overture looks forward — it previews what is to come — but it was written looking backward, from the vantage of a completed work. This is not deceptive. It is honest about how creation actually works: you often don't know what you were doing until you're done. The introduction is where you say, for the first time, what you meant all along.

    The same is often true of prefaces, introductions, opening paragraphs. Writers report returning to the beginning after finishing and finding that it is wrong — not factually but tonally, in what it promises, in the posture it assumes. The opening that seemed right before the work was written is frequently replaced by one that reflects the work as it actually turned out. The beginning is written in the past tense, pretending to be in the future. It is the one part of a work written with full knowledge of where it goes.

    performance

    the silence before the first note

    In orchestral performance, there is a specific silence before the overture begins: the conductor steps to the podium, raises the baton, the players lift their instruments, the hall stills. This silence is not absence. It is the moment when the audience shifts from distracted to prepared, from scattered to listening. It is the last moment in which the piece has not yet started, and there is something in this moment that cannot be recovered once it passes. The first note arrives into silence that has been prepared for it.

    Some composers have exploited the charged quality of this silence deliberately: a note so quiet it might be the continuation of the silence, so the piece seems to have been underway for some time before you realize it has started. Others begin with a sharp, loud chord that uses the prepared silence as kinetic energy, the contrast between the held breath and the sudden sound doing more work than the note alone could manage. The overture begins before the overture begins. The preparation is part of the content.
