vertigo

when the reference frame shifts

tilt: 0.0°
physical

the inner ear disagrees with the eyes

Clinical vertigo is a mismatch between sensory inputs. The vestibular system reports one thing; the visual system reports another; the brain, unable to reconcile them, produces the sensation of spinning. This is not an error in perception. It is an honest report of a genuine conflict. The world is stationary. The information about the world is not. This distinction — between the thing and the information about the thing — is where most vertigo begins.

conceptual

the belief you organized around turns out to be wrong

Not wrong in its details — wrong in its structure. You discover that the thing you used as a fixed point was itself moving. Everything you positioned relative to it needs to be repositioned. This takes longer than you expect. The mind looks for the next fixed point immediately, sometimes settling on one that is also moving. The vertigo is not in the wrongness. It is in the interval between the old orientation and the new one, when nothing is level and the tools you would use to find level are themselves suspect.

temporal

which version of you is real

You find a photograph from seven years ago. The person in it is clearly you. They are also clearly not you. You look at the face the way you might look at a stranger you recognize but cannot place. This is not dissociation; it is perspective. From here you can see that person's life arranged ahead of them, can see the events they do not know are coming, can see what they are about to lose. They look confident. The vertigo is not in the past. It is in the now, when you remember that someone is looking at you the same way, from somewhere you cannot see.

relational

the person turns out to be different

Not different in a discoverable way — you didn't misread the evidence. You read it correctly and drew a conclusion that the evidence supported. Then new evidence arrived and the conclusion required revision. The vertigo is not in being wrong about them. It is in discovering that the version of them you had built inside yourself is not the same as the person, and that the inside version, having lived with you, has accumulated meaning and context and history that the actual person does not share. You now have to grieve the inside version while recalibrating toward the actual one. These are different tasks.

narrative

the story you have been telling is revised

You were the one who stayed. Or the one who left. Or the one who tried. The story organized the events into something coherent and gave them a shape that allowed them to be filed away. Then something happens — a conversation, a document, a decade of distance — and the events are the same but the story is wrong. The facts don't change. The architecture changes. The vertigo is in re-sorting the same material into a different structure, knowing that this structure may also be provisional.

altitude

looking down at where you were

The vertigo at the top of something is not fear of falling. It is sudden awareness of scale. You can see from here how small the thing was that felt so large when you were in it. The argument, the worry, the period of time that seemed permanent. From altitude, the geography is visible all at once, including the parts that were hidden while you were inside them. This is useful. It is also slightly nauseating. To have been that small and not known it. To wonder, from here, what the altitude you can't yet see will reveal about where you are now.

Note: the page tilts as you scroll. This is intentional. The tilt is small — at most two degrees. If you noticed it, your vestibular system is functioning correctly. If you didn't, the information was received below the threshold of attention, which is where most orientation happens.
