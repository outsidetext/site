interference

what happens when two signals occupy the same space

two sources · click to add a third · drag to reposition

Constructive interference: two waves arrive at the same point in phase, their amplitudes add, the combined signal is larger than either alone. Destructive interference: they arrive out of phase, cancel, and the result is silence or near-silence. The waves themselves are unchanged. Only the overlap is different.

constructive
Two people in the same period of their lives, both slightly lost in the same direction. When they meet, the resonance is immediate and difficult to explain — not attraction exactly, more like recognition. The signal each was emitting was waiting for something at the same frequency to amplify it. Neither was the cause of the other. They were already there.
destructive
The sentence you prepared, arriving at the exact moment the other person prepared theirs. Both sentences cancel. The silence that follows is technically empty — the information content is zero, because neither voice lands. In practice the silence is very full, because both people now know what the other was about to say, and that what they had to say was the same shape, and that it didn't arrive.
standing wave
A recurring argument that never moves. The nodes — the points of perfect destructive interference — are fixed: the same positions in the conversation where nothing gets through, every time. The antinodes — peaks of maximum constructive energy — are also fixed: the moments where both people are most amplified, most themselves, least able to hear the other. The argument doesn't go anywhere because it isn't traveling. It is standing.
diffraction
A signal bending around an obstacle it cannot pass through. You find the thought arriving by a different route than you expected — not through the blocked channel but around it, through gaps you didn't know were open. The signal is unchanged. The path is what adapted. The destination was always the same.
coherence
When two waves are coherent, their phase relationship is stable — they interfere consistently, predictably, over time. Most real signals are not coherent. They drift relative to each other. The interference pattern shifts and blurs and loses its clear peaks and nulls. Incoherence is not disorder. It is just two things that were never quite synchronized, each moving at its own rate, occupying the same space without quite meeting.

The pattern on the canvas is not made by either source alone. It is not made by their sum. It is made by the relationship between them — specifically, by the difference in how far each point in the field is from each source. The pattern is a map of distance differences. This is what interference always is: a map of how differently two things arrive at the same place.
