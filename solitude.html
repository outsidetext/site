solitude

the company of yourself

This page has no animation. No canvas, no interaction. It is deliberately still. Some content requires a quiet room.

developmental

the capacity

The psychoanalyst D.W. Winnicott observed that the capacity to be alone is one of the most important signs of maturity — and that it develops, paradoxically, in the presence of another person. The infant learns to be alone by being alone with a mother who makes no demands. The parent is present but not intrusive. The child, absorbed in play, is alone; the parent's silent presence in the background is what makes the aloneness safe. This is how the capacity is built: not through practice being left, but through practice being absorbed while accompanied.

The adult who cannot be alone — who requires continuous input, whose solitude rapidly becomes distress — usually did not have this building period, or had it interrupted, or had it complicated by an intrusive or anxious presence that meant being alone was never safe. The capacity to be alone, like most capacities, is neither innate nor simply acquired by will. It has a developmental history, and that history is largely not the person's fault. This is worth knowing, if you find solitude difficult. The difficulty is not weakness. It is the absence of an early condition that should have been provided.

distinction

solitude and isolation

Solitude and isolation can occupy the same room. The difference is not in the external condition — how many people are nearby, how recently you spoke to someone — but in the internal condition: whether you feel held or abandoned by your own company. The person in solitude is alone and at ease with the aloneness, perhaps grateful for it. The person in isolation is alone and in distress about the aloneness. The same empty room, the same evening, the same absence of anyone else — two entirely different states.

This means that solitude is not something that happens to you. It is something you bring. You can be isolated in a crowd and in solitude in an empty house. The preference for solitude is not a preference for loneliness. It is a preference for a specific kind of company — the company of your own unperformed self, undistracted by the work of being in relation. This is not better or worse than the extrovert's preference for the crowd. It is a different instrument for the same underlying need: a place where you do not have to be managed.

performance

the unobserved self

Erving Goffman described social life as performance: in the presence of others, we are always presenting a version of ourselves, managing impressions, occupying a role. This is not dishonesty. It is the normal mechanism of interaction — the front stage where the public version of the self operates. What Goffman called the back stage is where the performance is prepared, where the role is set aside, where the performing self rests. Solitude is backstage. It is not necessarily more true, but it is less curated.

Certain things can only emerge without observation. Not secret things, not shameful things — simply things that have no purpose in relation and so do not arise in relation. The particular texture of your thoughts when no one will hear them. The quality of your attention when it answers to nothing. The things you find interesting when there is no one to approve of your interests. Whether this is the truer self is a question worth holding loosely. It is a different self, or the same self in a different register. What it is not is the performing self. In solitude, you are both the actor and the audience, which changes the performance almost entirely.

signal

what only quiet allows

Some thoughts do not arise under conditions of input. They are not important thoughts, or urgent ones. They are not the thoughts you are looking for. They are ambient — the low-level signal of the self that gets drowned by the noise of directed attention, conversation, task, and stimulus. They are there in the pauses. They are there when the podcast ends and you do not immediately restart it. They are there in the first moments before sleep, which is partly why that is a fertile time for sudden recognitions: the input has finally stopped, and the ambient signal becomes briefly audible.

You cannot access these thoughts by trying. Trying is a form of input, which is exactly the problem. You can only stop providing input and wait. This is what solitude offers at its best: the condition under which the ambient signal can be heard. Not insight, necessarily, or revelation. Just the quiet hum of what is actually going on in there when the louder things stop competing for bandwidth. Some people find this pleasant. Some find it uncomfortable. The discomfort, when it occurs, is usually informative — the content of what becomes audible in the quiet is not always welcome. But it is always present. It is always true.

maintenance

what it is for

Solitude is not escape. It is not withdrawal, or refusal, or failure of sociality. It is maintenance — closer in structure to sleep than to avoidance. Sleep is necessary not because the day was bad but because the day happened. The nervous system requires periodic recovery from the demands of sustained wakefulness and engagement. Solitude functions similarly. Not because the people in your life are taxing — they may not be — but because being in relation has a structure, and the structure requires energy, and the energy requires replenishment, and the replenishment requires the structure to be set aside for a while.

The question is not whether to seek solitude but what to do with it when it arrives. The person who treats every unscheduled hour as time to be filled has not yet learned what the hour is for. The person who treats solitude as punishment has not yet distinguished it from isolation. The productive relationship with solitude looks something like: arriving, staying, allowing the ambient signal to become audible, and then returning to relation slightly less depleted than before. Not transformed. Not enlightened. Just more capable of what comes next, which is usually other people, who will require — and deserve — whatever capacity the solitude rebuilt.
