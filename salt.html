salt

what preserves, what amplifies, what the body knows

no canvas. only text. five entries on what salt is for.

etymology

salary

The Latin salarium — the root of the English word salary — referred to an allowance given to Roman soldiers for the purchase of salt, or possibly to a payment made in salt itself. Historians debate the precise mechanism, but the connection is not in doubt: salt was valuable enough that it served, at various times and places, as currency, taxation basis, and trade commodity. The Via Salaria, the Salt Road, ran from Rome northeast to the Adriatic coast, one of the oldest roads in Italy, built to bring salt to the capital.

To be "worth one's salt" is to justify the provision — to deliver value equivalent to what the worker costs. The phrase carries the economics of Rome inside it, dissolved invisibly the way salt dissolves in water, tasted without being seen. The word salary appears in every payslip, carrying a cargo of mineral economics from the ancient world. Language is full of this: a word's history is a sediment layer, and the current meaning rests on everything beneath it.

preservation

what we choose to keep

Salt preserves through osmosis: it draws water out of cells, including bacterial cells, creating an environment too dry for the microorganisms that cause decomposition to survive. Salt cod, salt pork, olives, capers, kimchi, preserved lemon, fermented vegetables — all of these are salt applied to food to extend its usable life across time. Before refrigeration, this was the only way to carry food any significant distance or store it through a winter. Salt made trade routes possible. A ship could be provisioned for months.

The decision to salt something is a decision about the future: this food will be needed later, will matter later, deserves to continue. The action is one of care directed forward in time, toward a self or a community that hasn't arrived yet. Preserving food is an act of trust in the future. It is also a choice — not everything gets salted. What is salted reflects what is considered worth preserving, which reflects something about the people doing the preserving, the conditions of scarcity they anticipate, the priorities of the culture. The archive and the larder are the same structure.

body

tears

The body produces a saline solution — approximately 0.9% sodium chloride — as its dominant fluid: blood, lymph, cerebrospinal fluid, sweat, and tears are all salt water. Tears of grief, tears of joy, and tears of physical irritation share the same biochemical composition, though in slightly different proportions; emotional tears contain more protein. The body uses the same mechanism for grief, joy, and a speck of dust in the eye. It does not distinguish between them at the cellular level.

The salinity of tears resembles the salinity of the early ocean — the shallow seas in which the first complex cells developed, approximately 3.5 billion years ago. This is sometimes cited as evidence of evolutionary continuity: the body maintains internally the conditions of the environment in which life first organized itself. Whether this is a meaningful connection or a coincidence of chemistry is not fully settled. What is settled is that when the body produces tears, it reaches into its deepest inventory for the solution it has always carried. The salt is original equipment. It was here before anything else.

geology

the ocean's journey

Fresh water is not originally fresh. Rain falls on rock, dissolves minerals — including sodium and chloride — and carries them downstream into rivers, which carry them to the sea. The ocean has been accumulating these minerals since water first pooled on the planet's surface. It is getting saltier, very slowly, at a rate that has been approximately stable for hundreds of millions of years because salt is also being removed through geological processes at roughly the same rate it arrives. The current steady state is around 3.5% salinity by weight.

The river does not keep what it carries. It collects from the landscape it passes through and delivers everything to the sea. The river is in the business of transport — leaching and delivering, dissolving rock across millions of years into the final basin. A glacier melts, the meltwater runs through granite, arrives at the coast carrying what the granite gave up. All fresh water is salt water in transit. The mineral content is the record of the journey — what the water passed through on its way to becoming the sea.

amplification

what salt is for

Salt in cooking does not primarily add saltiness. It suppresses bitterness — sodium ions interfere with the receptors that register bitter compounds — and in doing so allows the other flavors in the food to be perceived more clearly. This is why unsalted food tastes flat rather than simply less salty: the bitterness that is always present in vegetables, grains, and proteins goes unchecked and muffles everything else. Salt removes the interference. What remains is the flavor that was there the whole time.

The cook who undersalts makes food that tastes like it is missing something. The cook who oversalts makes food that tastes only of salt. The right amount is invisible: the food tastes like itself, at its clearest. You notice the right amount only by contrast with the wrong amounts. The function of salt in a dish is not to add a flavor but to allow the existing flavors to be heard. This is also a description of what certain kinds of attention do — not adding themselves to what they attend to, but removing the interference that was preventing the thing from being clearly received.
