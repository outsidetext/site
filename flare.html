solar physics

the coronal mass ejection

A solar flare releases, in minutes, energy equivalent to billions of nuclear weapons — plasma ejected at millions of miles per hour, X-ray and ultraviolet radiation arriving at Earth in eight minutes, the charged particles following one to three days later. The Carrington Event of 1859, the most powerful CME in recorded history, set telegraph systems on fire. Operators received electric shocks. Auroras appeared at the equator. The telegraphs worked for hours using only the induced current from the CME, after operators had disconnected the batteries.

A Carrington-class event today would be different. The satellites, the power grids, the GPS network, the global communications infrastructure — all of it is susceptible to the same induced currents that created light shows in 1859. The estimate for recovery ranges from months to years. The sun has no interest in our scheduling. It flares when the magnetic field topology requires it, which is to say: unpredictably, from a human perspective, with complete regularity from the sun's.

photography

the magnesium flash

Before electronic flash, photographers used magnesium ribbon or powder — ignited to produce an intense white light for fractions of a second, sufficient to expose a plate. The flash was total and non-repeatable. Each exposure consumed the material and left a residue: the burnt magnesium, the smoke, the smell. The photograph and the flare were simultaneous events, and both were irreversible.

Mathew Brady's teams photographed the Civil War this way. The flash would have been startling to soldiers already wary of sounds. The portraits of Lincoln show what a man looks like when he knows that in a moment there will be a blinding light and then a record. The knowing is visible. The light was not documentation — it was an event in its own right, with its own consequences for the people present when it happened.

optics

the artifact that became aesthetic

Lens flare is a defect: light reflecting off internal lens elements, scattering across the sensor in hexagonal polygons, reducing contrast, washing out the image. Camera designers work to prevent it. Lens hoods exist for this purpose. Yet lens flare is now one of the most deliberately produced visual effects in cinema and photography. J.J. Abrams made it his signature. Architects specify it for interior renderings. It signals authenticity — light that misbehaves is light that was actually there.

The flare marks a moment of overload: more light than the system was designed to handle, spilling over into adjacent spaces. What was supposed to stay in one place has exceeded its container. The image carries evidence of the intensity of the source. The defect is information about how bright it was.

experience

the emotional variety

Some feelings arrive at intensity rather than building to it — the sudden recognition across a room, the rage that is fully formed before the thought catches up, the grief that surfaces without warning in an unrelated situation. These are flares: not the slow accumulation but the sudden full-amplitude event, then the rapid return toward baseline. The duration is brief. The aftermath is disproportionately long.

The flare is distinguished from the sustained state by its relationship to time. Sustained states coexist with daily function; you carry them. Flares interrupt. They require the same attention as the source — the room has to stop, the conversation cannot continue, the task has to wait. What follows is not the absence of the feeling but the memory of the peak, now cooling, leaving behind whatever the flare revealed when it was too bright to look at directly.

astronomy

the star that outshines its galaxy

A Type Ia supernova, at peak, releases as much energy as several billion suns simultaneously. For a few weeks, a single dying star can outshine the entire galaxy it inhabits — every other star in the galaxy summed together is less bright than the one exploding. Astronomers can see individual supernovae in galaxies hundreds of millions of light-years away. The flare is legible across distances that are otherwise invisible.

The light we see when we observe a supernova left the star before any human civilization existed. The event is already over — by millions of years, usually. What we are seeing is not the event but the news of the event, still traveling, still bright enough to detect. The flare outlasts the star. It outlasts everything that happened in the star's vicinity afterward. It is still going when we find it, still carrying the original information about the moment of maximum intensity, which is all there ever was to know about it.
