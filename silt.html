silt

what the digital life deposits, layer by layer

In geology, silt is what remains after the water moves on — the fine particles that settle when the current slows. The internet deposits its own sediment: accounts, formats, habits, grief. Each era compresses into a layer. Older layers grow harder, denser, more resistant to revival. The strata are arranged below, surface at top.

2022–now
surface
the accelerating present
Still soft, still warm. The layer is not yet compacted — it moves. Notifications still carry weight. Some links still resolve. The apps from this era run. The grief of this layer is not yet legible as grief, more like fatigue. A baseline loudness that makes silence feel like malfunction.
algorithmic feeds the group chat short-form video browser tabs, hundreds disappeared apps
2019–2021
2–4 cm
the closed years
A distinct layer, identifiable by its texture: the screen as sole window, the meeting as sole room. The artifacts of this era are domestic and digital simultaneously — a backdrop chosen for video calls, a subscription started and forgotten, the vocabulary of isolation that most people filed away without naming.
video call fatigue sourdough documentation the mute button ambient news grief twelve streaming services
2014–2018
5–9 cm
the saturation layer
When the social networks reached their full volume and everyone had an account and the performance of having-an-account became the thing itself. This layer is dense with engagement. Most of what settled here cannot be fully excavated — the metrics, the opinions, the photographs curated for an audience that has since dispersed. Still compressing.
the ratio screenshots as evidence the viral moment deleted tweets the like as communication
2008–2013
10–17 cm
the app era
The moment the internet moved into the pocket and became constant. Apps installed and deleted, data plans acquired, the gesture vocabulary of swipe and pinch that the hand now knows without instruction. This is also when the photograph became continuous — cameras that were always with you produced images of everything, which meant images of nothing in particular.
Blackberry passwords check-ins the original Instagram QR codes, first time snake, final version
2003–2007
18–28 cm
the social formation
The first time most people had a profile — a public facing self constructed of text, chosen music, and a profile picture selected with unusual care. The away message as art form. The wall post as correspondence. The friendship request as a legible social gesture. Most of this layer is inaccessible now: the servers have been redirected or shut down, the data not exported, the social self of this era legible only in memory.
the away message top eight the wall 43,000 songs, ripped GChat status
1997–2002
29–45 cm
the early web
When the internet was where you went, not where you were. Dial-up's connecting sound. Waiting for a photograph to load line by line. The homepage as aesthetic statement — tiled backgrounds, visitor counters, guestbooks where strangers left their traces. Web rings. Under construction GIFs. The sense that arriving somewhere on the internet was an event worth marking. Almost all of this is gone. Wayback Machine holds fragments. The experience is irretrievable.
GeoCities (38 million pages) the 56k modem web rings AIM screen names Napster, briefly
before 1997
bedrock
the primordial layer
Before most people had access: universities, defense networks, Usenet, BBS boards, MUDs. A layer so compressed that most of what it contains is structural — the protocols, the conventions, the assumptions that the layers above were built on and rarely examined. The gestures of this era live on invisibly: why email looks the way it does, why "you've got mail" was once astonishing, why the @ sign was waiting in the keyboard for exactly this purpose, unclaimed.
