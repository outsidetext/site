on things that do not stay put

physics

weightlessness and the missing floor

Astronauts in orbit do not float. They fall — continuously, permanently, at exactly the rate the Earth curves away beneath them. Weightlessness is not the absence of gravity; it is the absence of a surface to register your weight against. The sensation of floating is the body's report of having lost the floor, not the ceiling. On Earth, proprioception depends on pressure from below. Remove the floor and the body reports a new kind of presence: itself, in space, without a counterforce. The inner ear finds no up. The stomach lifts.

This is why the sensation of falling and the sensation of floating are so similar: they are the same physical event, at different altitudes of panic. What separates them is the presence or absence of the ground approaching. In orbit there is no approaching ground. There is only the falling, and then more falling, and then the realization that this is now the condition, and the body learning, slowly, to call it something else.

maritime

the message in the bottle

In 1914, a fisherman named Bjarne Iversen found a bottle in the waters near Norway. Inside was a postcard from a German oceanographer named Georg von Neumayer, who had released it from a ship in 1886 to study the movement of ocean currents. Twenty-eight years of drift. The bottle had traveled approximately 1,000 miles, which works out to roughly 0.1 miles per day — slower than most snails, and yet it arrived. Neumayer had died in 1909, five years before anyone answered his implicit question about where the water went.

The message in the bottle is the most committed form of sending: it releases control of destination, arrival, recipient, and timing simultaneously. Most communication involves at least one of these being managed. The bottle gives up all of them. The sender decides what to say and where to put it in the water. Everything after that is current, wind, and whatever stranger finds the beach at the right moment. The oceanographers who still use this method do so not because it is precise, but because it is honest: it measures where the water actually goes, not where a model says it should.

linguistics

the free-floating signifier

In semiotics, a floating signifier is a word that has become unmoored from a stable referent — still in use, still carried through conversation, but no longer attached to a fixed meaning. The French anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss called it a "zero-value symbol": a placeholder that can mean almost anything, which is another way of saying it means almost nothing in particular. Words like "energy," "freedom," "authentic," and "natural" have mostly entered this category. They persist in language because they are still legible as signals of certain kinds of feeling, even though the content is no longer stable enough to argue about.

The float is not always a failure. Some words need to drift to stay available. A word too tightly pinned to its referent becomes brittle — it breaks when the referent changes, and something that once did real semantic work becomes an artifact or a technical term. The floating word survives by accommodating its context. The cost is precision. The benefit is continued circulation. Language keeps using the word the way a current keeps carrying the bottle — not because it knows where it's going, but because something is still in motion and still readable to anyone who finds it.

anatomy

the floating rib

The eleventh and twelfth ribs are called "floating ribs" — not metaphorically but technically. The first seven ribs attach at the sternum in front. Ribs eight, nine, and ten attach through cartilage. But eleven and twelve connect only at the spine behind, their anterior ends free, unattached to anything but muscle and tissue. They protect the kidneys and the lower back and the spleen through their presence alone, without being anchored to the front of the structure they are guarding. In anatomy, "floating" is the word for a bone that is not fixed at both ends. It is a structural description, not a symptom.

There are people for whom the floating rib causes pain — a condition called slipping rib syndrome, where the cartilage at the end of a lower rib catches on adjacent structures as the body moves. The rib that was meant to float catches. The treatment in severe cases is to remove it: the floating rib excised, the body continuing without it. What looks like structural freedom can become a source of friction. The unanchored thing that was meant to move freely finds that freedom is not the same as ease.

psychology

the untethered self

Dissociation is sometimes described by those who experience it as floating. Not a pleasant float — not weightlessness as release — but the sense of observing oneself from a slight distance, the self become a thing watched rather than inhabited. The body continues its activity. The person watches from a position slightly removed, a few inches behind their own eyes, or above the scene, as if the cable connecting selfhood to experience has gone slack. The clinical term is depersonalization; the experience is being the observer of your own floating.

Sensory deprivation tanks are designed to produce a different version — floating deliberately induced, the body suspended in salt water, sound and light removed, the signal of proprioception quieted until the boundary between self and water becomes approximate. People report the floating there as releasing rather than untethering — the self not diminished but somehow relieved of the maintenance that ordinary embodiment requires. The same physical state, the same absence of counterforce from below, experienced as two different things depending on whether you chose it. Float tanks and dissociation occupy the same sensory description. Their difference is almost entirely about consent.
