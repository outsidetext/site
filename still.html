etymology

the two meanings

Old English stille meant quiet, motionless — a thing arrested, at rest, not in motion. The adverbial sense came later, built from the same root but traveling a different path: still as in continuing, persisting, yet. Both meanings carry the same weight of time, but differently. The adjective stops it; the adverb crosses it. One says: here. The other says: still here.

What is remarkable is that they share a source in the sense of something that goes on without interruption. The adjective stayed where it was. The adverb kept moving. They parted at some point in the history of the language and developed in opposite directions, and yet they kept the same name. English held both in the same word, perhaps because it understood something: that stillness and continuation are not opposites. They are the same root reaching in two directions.

photography

the still image

The photograph arrests the moment. It extracts a slice of time at the thickness of a shutter speed — a fraction of a second held against the rest of time's flowing. The subject was in motion and did not know they were being stilled. They were reaching for something, or laughing at something, or looking at something that is now off-frame and unknowable. The photograph holds the reaching. It holds the laugh. It does not hold whatever they were looking at.

But the people in photographs are also still alive, in another sense — still present somewhere in the world, still in motion, still reaching for things and laughing at things. The photograph makes the past present and keeps it there, but the person in the photograph refuses to stay there. They have moved on. They are still. The image was stilled; the subject kept going. This is the quiet vertigo of looking at old photographs: the two meanings of the word sitting in the same frame, not resolving.

water

still waters

The phrase contains both meanings simultaneously: water that does not move, and water that continues beneath the surface. Still water is not stopped water. Evaporation continues. Convection circulates the temperature. Organisms live and die in it. Sediment drifts through the column toward the bottom, settling in layers that will eventually constitute a kind of record. What looks motionless from above is in constant microscopic motion. The stillness is a surface condition.

The still pond reflects the sky, which is itself in motion — clouds crossing, light changing, the slow wheel of the day. The reflection moves. The water, to the eye, does not. This is why still water has always seemed profound to those who looked into it: not because it is calm, but because it holds a moving world in perfect stillness, and the two conditions coexist without contradiction. The water is still. The world it reflects is not. Still it holds it.

continuation

still here

There is something in the phrase "I'm still here" that no other construction quite captures. Still here is not the same as here. Here is a location; still here is a feat. The still carries the entire weight of persistence — against time, against the forces that work against remaining, against whatever might have intervened. It says: I could have stopped being here. I did not stop. That gap — between when you might have ceased and the present moment, which you have successfully crossed — is what the word holds open.

Said at the end of a hard year, it is a kind of accounting. Said in answer to a worried question, it is reassurance. Said in the dark to no one, it is the most honest thing a person can say about their own survival — not triumphant, not resigned, but precise. A report from the field. I am still here. The word still is doing all the work of the sentence. The here almost doesn't matter. It is the still that means something.

distillation

the still

The apparatus that concentrates by removing: apply heat, collect what rises, let the impure fall away. A still is a device for making something more itself. It works by knowing that most of a thing is not the thing — is water, or impurity, or what was added rather than what was essential. The still separates them. What remains is more concentrated, more accurate, more purely what it was always trying to be.

The action is the noun: to still is to distill. You still a thing to make it what it most is. The etymology connects here to the adjective — the still point, the arrested motion, the thing made quiet so that what remains can be identified. Distillation requires stillness; you cannot separate what is in motion. The still is the instrument that makes stillness productive. Something is removed. Something is kept. What is kept is the truth of the original, at higher proof.

both

simultaneously

The deepest reading of the word holds both senses at once, without choosing. You are still — not moving, not needing to move, at rest in the body and the moment. And you are still here — continuing, persisting, present against the odds and the passage of time. The stillness is not absence of action. It is the action of remaining. To be still and to still be are not in tension. They collapse into each other at a certain depth of the word.

The still point of the turning world. Eliot found it: the place where motion and motionlessness meet, where the dance is but neither arrest nor movement. Not immovable — stillness is not rigidity. Not finished — continuation is not achievement. Both, simultaneously, at the still point. The word is a hinge between two states that language usually treats as opposites. English put them in the same word by accident or by wisdom, and the word has been doing both things ever since. Still. It has not stopped. It is still going.
