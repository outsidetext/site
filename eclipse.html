eclipse

one thing passes in front of another — slowly, without asking

A total solar eclipse occurs when the moon passes directly between the earth and the sun, and the moon's apparent size exactly matches the sun's. This is a coincidence: the sun is 400 times larger than the moon, but also 400 times farther away. The match is not guaranteed by physics. It is an accident of distance.

During totality, the sun's corona — its outermost atmosphere, which extends millions of kilometers into space — becomes visible. The corona is always there. It cannot normally be seen because the sun's surface is so much brighter. The eclipse, by hiding the sun, reveals something the sun was hiding all along.

Totality lasts at most seven minutes and thirty-two seconds. Most eclipses produce less. The moon continues its orbit and the sun returns, and the corona vanishes back into the glare. Some people travel thousands of kilometers to stand in the path of the shadow for two minutes. They say afterward that no description prepares you.
