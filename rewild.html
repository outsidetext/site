rewild

the return to untamed states; what happens when the fences come down

drifting freely
ecology

trophic cascades

In 1995, wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone after a seventy-year absence. Within a decade, the rivers had changed. Not because wolves built anything — because wolves hunted elk, and elk stopped grazing the riverside vegetation, and the vegetation grew back, and the roots held the banks, and the banks stopped eroding, and the rivers slowed and widened and their courses shifted. The wolves had rewilded the rivers without going near them.

This is a trophic cascade: the effect of one species reverberating through the ecosystem in ways that are difficult to predict in advance and obvious in retrospect. The system was not missing wolves; it was missing the fear of wolves, which changes how elk move, which changes what gets eaten, which changes what grows, which changes the ground, which changes the water. Rewilding doesn't restore a fixed past state — it reinstates the processes that create changing states. The result is not what was there before. It is something alive in a way that managed systems are not.

cognition

the default mode

When the brain is not directed toward a task, it doesn't go quiet. A network activates — the default mode network — involved in mind-wandering, self-referential thought, imagining the future, and making sense of other people. This is not idling. The metabolic cost of the default mode is nearly the same as focused work. The brain is always doing something; the question is only what kind of something.

Directed attention is a cultivated capacity, like a domesticated garden: shaped, productive, resource-expensive to maintain. The default mode is the wildness underneath — the system that runs when the tending stops. There is evidence that the creative insights which feel sudden arrive from this state: the problem worked on consciously and then abandoned, the answer surfacing during a walk or a shower. The wild state does a kind of work that the directed state cannot. You can't schedule it. You can only stop mowing and see what comes back.

loss

what doesn't return

Not everything rewilded becomes a trophic cascade. Some things that were tamed have lost the instinct. The Père David's deer, extinct in the wild for thousands of years, reintroduced to China in the twentieth century: they had to learn to fear predators, because the prey response had atrophied across generations of captivity. Some domesticated plants no longer know how to disperse their own seeds — they depended on human planting for so long that the dispersal structures have disappeared. The wildness is not waiting underneath the domestication. It was the domestication.

A person who has lived for a long time with a heavily managed interior — structured days, controlled responses, cultivated outputs — may find that extended unstructured time produces not the expected flowering but disorientation and anxiety. The capacity for wildness requires maintenance too, in its own way. You can't switch it off for years and find it instantly available. The rewilding of a person is slower than it sounds, and the grief is real: not only for what was lost to management but for the wildness that didn't wait.

structure

load-bearing taming

Some of what has been domesticated cannot be rewilded without consequences that are not acceptable. The river that was channeled to prevent flooding of the downstream city: rewild it and recover the wetlands, lose the city. The trade is real. The environmental case for rewilding the river is strong and does not change the fact that there are people who live there. This is not an argument against rewilding — it is an argument for being precise about what is load-bearing in any given case, which requires looking clearly at what is propped up by the managed state.

The same question applies to interior rewilding. Some of the management is protective — the emotional regulation strategy that prevents a kind of collapse, the controlled presentation that maintains a relationship or a job or a safety. Removing all of it at once is not liberation; it is destabilization. The work is selective: identifying which taming is protective and which is suppressive, which fence is holding something out and which is holding something in, which structure is load-bearing and which is simply habit. The honest answer requires looking at what falls if the fence comes down.
