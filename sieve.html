mathematics

the sieve of Eratosthenes

Around 240 BCE, Eratosthenes described a method for finding all prime numbers up to a given limit: write out all integers from 2 to N. Mark 2 as prime, then eliminate all its multiples. Move to the next unmarked number (3), mark it prime, eliminate its multiples. Continue until every number has been either marked prime or eliminated. What remains after the sieve passes through the list is exactly the primes — the numbers that passed through every filter because they have no divisors other than 1 and themselves.

The sieve works because it is not looking for primes directly. It is systematically removing non-primes. What's left is not chosen — it is what survived. This negative definition of the primes turns out to be computationally efficient for small numbers and philosophically interesting for all numbers: the primes are not a type with a positive defining characteristic so much as a residue of the eliminations. They are what the composite numbers aren't.

archaeology

what survives excavation

Archaeological excavation is, in part, a sieving operation. The matrix of soil is removed in controlled units and passed through fine-mesh screens. What passes through is discarded; what remains is recorded, identified, catalogued. The screen determines what is legible: a 6mm mesh passes seeds, retains potsherds. A 2mm mesh retains fish bones. The archaeologist is only as informed as the mesh allows.

What this means is that the archaeological record is not the past — it is the past filtered through a series of decisions about what deserved recovery. The seed assemblage of a site tells you something about the diet. Whether it tells you accurately depends on what kind of seeds were systematically missed because no one thought to use a fine enough screen. Archaeological knowledge is always knowledge of what the sieve retained, not of what passed through it, and the two are not the same.

memory

what the mind keeps

Memory is a sieve, but an inconsistent one: it retains by emotional charge rather than by importance, by repetition rather than by precision, by novelty rather than by frequency. The most emotionally significant events tend to produce flashbulb memories — vivid, detailed, confidently held — which turn out, when tested, to be no more accurate than ordinary memories. The confidence is not evidence of fidelity. The brightness of the recording is not proportional to its accuracy.

What passes through: most of Tuesday. The name of the person who smiled at you on a train. The route you drove a hundred times. What remains: the moment before the diagnosis, the specific light in the room the first time you understood something clearly, the exact words of the thing said that couldn't be unsaid. The sieve of memory seems to select for intensity, which is a rational strategy for a finite system managing infinite input. The cost is that the low-intensity past — most of what actually happened — is continuously discarded.

editing

the editorial function

An editor is a human sieve. The manuscript passes through and the editor eliminates what doesn't belong: the inconsistency, the overexplanation, the scene that was written to discover what came next and should not appear in the version that already knows. The good editor doesn't add. The good editor identifies what can be removed without changing what the work is doing, and removes it.

The sieve is not neutral — every sieve embeds a theory. The editor's theory about what the work is trying to do determines what is retained and what falls through. If the theory is wrong, the sieve destroys the work. If it's right, what remains after the passes is denser, more legible, and closer to the essential thing. The goal is a structure fine enough to catch what matters and coarse enough to let go of everything else, and the difficulty is that the categories are not decided in advance.

perception

what reaches consciousness

Of the approximately eleven million bits of information your sensory system processes per second, roughly forty reach conscious awareness. The gap is the sieve: a cascade of filters selecting what is novel, threatening, relevant to current goals, or sufficiently anomalous to require deliberate attention. The rest is processed and discarded below the threshold of awareness, which is why you don't notice the feeling of your clothes against your skin until you think about it.

The implication is that consciousness is not a window on the world. It is a narrow channel at the end of a very aggressive filtering process. What arrives at awareness is what the pre-conscious filters decided was worth escalating. The decisions were made without your consent or knowledge, by processes that have no interest in philosophical accuracy. You experience the output of the sieve as experience itself, without seeing the sieve, or the vast majority of what it discarded.
