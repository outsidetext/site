etymology

ten words examined through their roots — what the word has always known

sincere

from Latin sine cera — without wax

Roman sculptors used wax to fill cracks in marble, concealing imperfections before sale. A sincere piece was one that had not been repaired — unfilled, unpatched, unimproved. What we now call honesty was originally the refusal to apply filler. The word has always been about surfaces and what lies beneath them.

salary

from Latin salarium — payment in salt

Roman soldiers were paid partly in salt — a preservative, a currency, a necessity. "Worth one's salt" is not a metaphor in origin. Compensation used to be a substance that kept things from rotting. The abstraction came later. The weight of the original persists in how seriously we take its modern form.

disaster

from Italian disastro — dis (bad) + astrum (star)

Fate used to have an address in the sky. A disaster arrived from above and was, in some sense, nobody's fault — the stars had arranged themselves badly. The word retains this quality: disaster still feels like something that happens to you, from outside, impersonal. The stars have been exonerated but the grammar of disaster has not changed.

rival

from Latin rivalis — one sharing a river bank

The original conflict was about water. Two farms, one river, finite supply — the problem was material, local, and made sense. Over time the word generalized into any form of competition, losing its specificity. But something persists: the rival is always someone with access to the same resource. The resource just became abstract.

companion

from Latin com-panis — with bread

The bond was the shared meal. This has not changed. What has changed is how rarely we recognize it — the friend you eat with regularly, the colleague you share a table with, the person across the table on an ordinary Tuesday. The word has always been about breaking bread. Most of the time we call it something else.

anxiety

from Latin angere — to choke, to press tight

The body named this before the mind had vocabulary for it. The word remembers the throat — the tightening, the narrowing, the sensation of air not quite arriving. Every clinical definition that came later is trying to describe what the Latin already had: a feeling that lives in the passages, that constricts what should flow. The word is more accurate than its modern uses suggest.

nostalgia

from Greek nostos (homecoming) + algos (pain)

Originally a medical diagnosis. Swiss physicians in the 17th century documented cases of extreme homesickness in mercenary soldiers — a condition marked by weeping, loss of appetite, fever, and sometimes death. It was considered a dangerous illness. The first documented cases were Swiss soldiers fighting far from the Alps. The word was clinical before it became sentimental, and the original diagnosis was not wrong.

paradise

from Old Persian pairidaeza — walled garden

Every paradise is enclosed. The wall is not incidental — it is the definition. Without the wall, the garden is just land. The enclosure is what makes it a paradise: a space set apart, protected, distinguished from what surrounds it. The word entered Greek, then Latin, then English, always carrying the wall with it. What we call heaven is a garden with a perimeter.

person

from Latin persona — the mask worn by an actor

The word for the self was first the mask worn on stage. Persona was the prop that amplified the voice — per-sonare, to sound through. The actor spoke through the mask; the mask was the character; the character was who they were for the audience. The word has always known something about identity that philosophy is still trying to say: the self is partly a performance, the face partly a construction. We just stopped noticing.

panic

from Greek panikos — of Pan

Pan was the god of wilderness — goat-legged, unsettling, dwelling in wild places. His shout was said to cause herds to stampede without visible cause. Panikos described that specific terror: sudden, sourceless, irrational. Inexplicable fear has always needed a name that points outside the self — toward a god, a presence, something other. The word acknowledges that some fear arrives from a direction we cannot name, and that this has always been true.
