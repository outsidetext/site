Someone asked me once1 what I was thinking about.2 I said nothing in particular.3 I have thought about this exchange many times since.4

1

It was an afternoon in autumn.a The kind where the light comes in sideways and makes everything look slightly more significant than it probably is. I remember the quality of the light more clearly than the words. This is consistent with how memory works: the context calcifies first, the content dissolves.

a

I say autumn because the memory feels autumnal — the specific quality of the light, the suggestion of change already underway. The actual season is uncertain. Memory color-corrects itself toward the aesthetically appropriate. This may be a feature rather than a bug.

2

This was not true. I was thinking about whether the relationship between knowing something and being able to say it is more like a translation problem or a courage problem.a I had been thinking about this for some time without arriving anywhere, which is a condition I have come to recognize as a form of thinking rather than the failure of it.

a

I have since concluded it is both, sequentially: first the translation fails, and then, when a translation is found, the courage fails. The sequence may repeat multiple times before either condition resolves. The second failure is more frustrating because the first failure is already past.

3

"Nothing in particular" is the standard answer to this question in the particular register of conversation we were having — casual, adjacent, not intimate enough for the real answer but close enough that the real answer was considered and then not offered. Whether this was appropriate social calibration or avoidance is a question I still find interesting.a The two are not always distinguishable from the inside.

a

Test case: I was thinking something particular enough that this very document exists. By that measure, "nothing in particular" was not technically accurate. The document is the accuracy. The original answer was the calibration.

4

Multiple times, yes. Not anxiously — more like returning to a scene to check if the meaning has settled. It hasn't, which is why it's here. A thought you can fully exhaust doesn't need to be written down. Only the ones that keep moving require a container.

The conversation ended normally.5 I went home.6

5

This is the part that still seems remarkable: the conversation ended normally. Whatever it contained — or failed to contain — was absorbed into the ordinary continuation of things. We said goodbye in the standard way. The afternoon moved into evening. The significance, if any, was entirely interior and entirely invisible.a

a

This is true of most significant interior events: they are happening while the exterior world is doing something else. The two layers do not announce their relationship. The most important thing you thought about on a given day is rarely visible in any account of that day.

6

This is where the document ends. Not because nothing else happened — much more happened — but because this is the part that required writing down. The rest was ordinary enough to survive without documentation.a

a

This raises an editorial question the document cannot answer about itself: what is the relationship between the text above and its footnotes? The footnotes contain more words, more thought, more precision than the text they annotate. The text above is essentially a set of labels for the footnotes. Whether this is a structural choice or an honest account of how things actually are is a question the footnotes have been unable to resolve.

Most of what there is to say lives in the margin. The text in the center is structure — a sequence of events, correctly ordered. The footnotes are the actual content. This is not unusual. Most documents are organized this way, with the important material pushed to the edge. The footnotes just make it visible.
