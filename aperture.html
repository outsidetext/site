aperture

the size of the opening

A camera aperture controls how much light enters the lens. A smaller f-number means a wider opening; a larger f-number means a smaller one. Depth of field changes with it. So does what you can hold in focus simultaneously. These are also descriptions of a state of attention.

f/1.4
wide open

Everything floods in simultaneously. The periphery is present and undifferentiated. Sharp focus falls somewhere you didn't choose — a sound, an expression, a detail that wouldn't have mattered on a different day. The background blurs into something like emotion rather than information.

This is the setting of early mornings before the filters are up. Of unexpectedly charged encounters. Of arriving somewhere for the first time when you're tired enough that the usual defenses haven't engaged. You don't have control over what comes into focus. You have control only over whether to keep the aperture open, and for how long.

f/2.8
open

A generous depth of field. Two or three things can be sharp simultaneously. The background is soft but present; you can feel it there. You can see enough to navigate without losing the atmosphere.

This is the setting of good days — focused enough to accomplish things, open enough to notice what's alongside. Most working hours operate here by intention. Some conversations arrive here without planning, and those tend to be the ones worth remembering.

f/5.6
standard

Ordinary working vision. Most things at normal distance are reasonably sharp; the extremes fall away. You miss things at the edges. The image is functional and clear.

This is the setting most people operate in most of the time, and most people think of it as "seeing clearly" rather than as a particular setting at all. That is the nature of the default: it makes itself invisible. You don't notice it's a setting until you move away from it.

f/11
closed

A narrowed aperture. Depth of field increases paradoxically — more things appear technically sharp, but everything has the same flat, even illumination. The image is accurate but feels evacuated of atmosphere.

This is the setting of depletion, when nuance requires energy that isn't available. Also of certain high-functioning focuses — the surgeon's, the editor's, the person with a real deadline. Useful in doses. The flatness is a cost, not a feature. Everything is equally in focus, which means nothing is particularly present.

f/22
minimum

Almost nothing passes through. What remains: one thing, necessary. No atmosphere. No context. The background is not blurred — it is gone. The frame contains only what must be contained.

This setting appears in emergencies and in extremity — the first seconds after certain news, the moment of a decision that can't be walked back. It also appears at the end of long simplification, when things that were not essential have gradually been let go. At f/22 you can see very far. There is only one thing in the frame. Sometimes that is the right number of things.

The aperture is not fixed. It changes with the hour, the light, the level of available rest. The interesting question is not what setting you prefer — it's what setting you're actually at, right now, and whether that's the one you'd choose.
