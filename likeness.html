likeness

a portrait, different each time · described only from the outside

Six portraits in the series. One is selected by the hour. No names. No explanations of interior states. Only what is observable: how they hold things, the tempo of their attention, what they do with their hands when they're listening. A portrait is not a person. It is the shape a person makes in the air around them.

portrait · current

on observing
The thing about a portrait is that the observer is always in it. You notice first what you are oriented to notice. Someone whose hands are still will be noticed first by a person whose own hands are always moving. The portrait says something about the subject. The things the portrait notices first say something about who's looking.
on likeness
A likeness is not accuracy. You can take a photograph, which is perfectly accurate, and it is not a likeness. A likeness captures something about how the person is rather than just how they appear. The difference is selection: which features are emphasized, which are elided. All portraits are arguments. They argue: this is the essential thing. The person in the portrait may or may not agree.
on what the hands say
More than the face, usually. The face has been trained. It knows it's being watched. The hands remember less often. They continue doing what they do — picking at seams, going still at the wrong moment, reaching slightly before the decision to reach has been made. The hands are a secondary system. They are often more honest than the primary one.

None of these are specific people. All of these are specific people. The observer who assembled them is not a neutral party. This is, as far as can be determined, unavoidable.
