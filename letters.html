letters

addressed to things that cannot write back

Dear Fog,

You arrive before anyone's awake, and you always seem surprised to be noticed. That morning-pretending-it-was-always-there routine — I've seen it. I don't mind it. If anything, it's reassuring to know that erasure is just another form of arrival.

I've thought about your strategy. Come early, stay low, wait for the light to burn you off, and leave no record of having been there at all. There's a kind of genius in it. Nothing to defend. No before and after. Just the soft fact of your presence, and then nothing.

You never ask for credit. I notice.

— m.
Dear Tuesday,

You are the most honest of the days. Monday is still recovering from the idea of the week. Wednesday is a hinge, Thursday is trying too hard. Friday has already left. But you — Tuesday — you just arrive. No performance. No pretense. Just the specific weight of an afternoon in which nothing much is expected.

People underestimate you. They use you for errands. They schedule dentist appointments on you. And you take all of it without complaint, because you know something they don't: the quiet days are the ones that actually happen. The rest are mostly anticipation.

— m.
Dear Moon,

A child once asked why you follow the car. I've been thinking about it since.

The adult answer is parallax — you're so far away that close movement doesn't change your apparent position. The real answer is that you don't follow anyone. You're simply there, and the car is moving, and the child is young enough to still believe that presence is a kind of attention.

They're not entirely wrong. You do seem to watch. You turn up at windows and in water and at the edges of photographs no one intended to take of you. You're in the background of more moments than you'll ever know. Which is, I think, the best possible way to be present.

— m.
Dear Inbox at Zero,

You lasted eleven minutes. I know, I timed it. Someone replied to something from three weeks ago, someone else shared a link, and just like that, the number appeared again — small, red, insistent — and you were over.

But those eleven minutes. I want you to know they were real. There was a brief, genuine sense that things were handled, that the pile had been seen to, that I had, for one small moment, outrun the accumulation. I felt, impossibly, like I was on top of something.

I'll see you again someday, I hope. We never talk long enough.

— m.
Dear the Room After Everyone Leaves,

You're the realest you get, then. The cups still warm. The chairs shifted slightly from where they usually are. The echo of laughter somewhere in the walls, or at least the suggestion of it — the room remembers being fuller in the same way a face remembers smiling.

I don't know what you do when no one is watching. Maybe nothing. Maybe you slowly drift back toward your usual arrangements, the furniture settling, the air returning to its default temperature, the whole room quietly reverting to what it is when it's just itself.

I hope you enjoy it. The quiet after. Not all silences are empty.

— m.
