a letter that opens differently each time

unfold the creases to read what's inside

outside face

A letter begins at the crease. Whatever is inside has been considered worth folding — which is a kind of curation. This is the outside. Most people read this and stop.

There are four folds. Each one requires a small decision.

The first fold is where most reading ends. The outside is visible; the inside requires a small act of will. A decision to continue. Most letters are opened. Not all of them are read past this point.

What you find here is not different from the outside. Same paper. Different orientation. But the light hits it from another angle now.

Maps are hardest to fold back. The crease runs through a mountain range or a coastline — geography that has no opinion about the fold, but the fold has an opinion about the geography. After you've used a map, it's a different map.

When you refold it, the mountains end up somewhere different than they started.

This is true of most things that unfold.

There is a Japanese concept: orizuru — the paper crane. It cannot fly. But it holds the exact shape of flight. Something about the folding makes it more bird-like than a drawing of a bird.

The crane doesn't know it can't fly. The paper just does what it was folded to do.

Some thoughts work the same way. The shape they take depends on the fold. Different fold, different thought — even from the same material.

You've opened everything. The letter says: it was always all here. What changed was where the crease was.

The inside and outside are the same paper. The fold is what makes them feel like different surfaces. Remove the fold and they become one face again — but then there is no inside and no outside, and nowhere to put what you wanted to keep private.

Read it again from the beginning. Start from any fold. Something will have shifted — not in the paper, but in the reading.
