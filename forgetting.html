forgetting

what goes first — the mechanics and mercy of not remembering

some of this has already started to go

sequence

what goes first

The name, usually. Then the face loses its precision and becomes a general type — dark-haired, or quiet, or someone who laughed in a specific register you no longer hear clearly. The feeling goes last. You will forget the name and the face and still feel, for years, the particular quality of being in that person's presence — the texture of what it was like to be seen by them, or avoided by them, or anticipated by them. The body stores what the mind releases.

The order of forgetting is not arbitrary. It follows a logic the brain does not announce. Peripheral details first. Context next. Then the specific words, replaced by their approximate meaning. The emotional core last, and sometimes never.

mercy

the benevolent kind

There is a version of forgetting that is not failure. The exact content of most arguments is gone within a week; what remains is the emotional residue, minus the specific provocation — which is the useful part, the part you might actually learn from. The pain of early grief diminishes in ways the early grief could not imagine; this is not betrayal of the grief's object, it is the body being merciful to the self that must continue.

Forgetting is the mechanism by which the past does not simply accumulate until it fills all available space. Some of this is selection. Some of it happens without your participation. Both are necessary. You cannot keep everything and also keep going.

the opposite

what won't leave

There are things the forgetting cannot reach. Not because they are important — importance and persistence are different variables — but because of the conditions under which they were encoded. High arousal. Surprise. The particular vulnerability of certain hours. The moment carries an intensity that the brain logs as requiring permanent retention, which the brain sometimes misapplies.

The scene replays with the vividness of the day it happened, years later, while more significant events have blurred to outlines. The intrusive memory is not proof of importance. It is proof of the conditions that were present when it formed. The brain is a good recorder. It is a poor editor.

language

the word that went

You had a word for it. The exact word. It was precisely right, it arrived once, and you used it, and then it was gone — not gradually, not retrievable with effort, but gone in the way of a name on the tip of the tongue that then slips back and cannot be found, except this is worse because you do not remember that you had it, only that the exact thing you meant to say is not available in any translation you have found since.

The word may come back at 3am. It may arrive in a different language where you cannot use it. It may [the rest of this has gone]

selective

the pattern of what stays

What you remember is not a sample. It is a selection made by processes you did not supervise. The selection reveals something — what you attended to, what you feared, what you wanted, what surprised you. The gap between what happened and what you remember is a map of your inner weather at the time of the happening.

Two people present at the same event remember different events. Neither is wrong. Both are accurate records of a partial perception filtered through a particular self. Memory is autobiography, not transcription. The difference is everything.

self

the version of you that was forgotten

There is a self from nine years ago that you cannot fully access. You remember facts about them — what they were doing, what they were worried about. But you cannot get back inside their experience. The fear that felt total feels, at this distance, smaller than it was, or larger, but not right. The joy that was ordinary is now visible as extraordinary and is therefore falsified by the distance that makes it visible.

You are forgetting this version of yourself continuously. The person you are now will, eventually, be as inaccessible as the person from nine years ago. This is not frightening. It is how you are able to change.

technology

what the photograph remembers instead

The photograph is a record of surfaces at a moment. It does not record what you were thinking, or the smell of the room, or what had been said an hour before that had changed the quality of everything. It records the light. It records that everyone was present and appeared to be in certain configurations. Over time, the photograph displaces the memory — its clean surfaces replacing the blurred and feeling-laden record the mind was keeping.

You remember the photograph now more than you remember the event. The photograph has excellent recall. It remembers nothing that matters.

gift

what forgetting makes possible

If you remembered everything, you would be unable to read a book twice, hear a piece of music fresh, be surprised by someone you know. The first time anything happens is only the first time because the subsequent times have not yet made it familiar. Forgetting restores the possibility of the first time.

There is a name for the state in which every previous experience is fully present and inaccessible to being forgotten. It does not sound like a gift. It is described, by those who have it, as a curse. The mercy of forgetting is not a consolation for a limitation. It is its own kind of grace.
