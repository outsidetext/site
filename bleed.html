bleed

where one thing becomes another

In printing, bleed is the area beyond the trim edge — content that extends past the boundary to prevent white margins. You include more than necessary so the cut falls cleanly. Most containment works this way.

ink into paper

the first kind

Ink is not deposited on paper; it is absorbed into it. The fibers draw it inward by capillary action. The degree of bleed — called "feathering" — determines how sharp the edge of a letter appears and whether the white of the page is preserved around the stroke. On uncoated stock the ink spreads. On coated stock it sits at the surface. The most precise printing is the most controlled bleed, which is not the same as no bleed. Something always crosses the line.

emotion into the next day

the residue of yesterday

You go to sleep with something unresolved. You wake to what feels, briefly, like a clean state. Then it bleeds back through. Not all of it — not the full acuity of the night's version — but a residue, a stain in the shape of the feeling, which colors the morning without being the feeling itself. This is different from rumination. Rumination is circular. This is osmotic. The membrane between yesterday and today is not impermeable. Whatever was most saturated will cross it.

person into their work

contained the unrequested autobiography

Every made thing contains the person who made it in excess of what they intended to include. The choices that felt neutral — the pace, the syntax, the places where the attention dwelled longest — these are not neutral. They are a record of the maker's preoccupations at the time of making. The work was supposed to be about the subject. It is also about the maker. This is not failure of craft. Craft is the attempt to control the bleed, not eliminate it. Some makers learn to use it.

the past into now

what the previous arrangement left behind

You leave a relationship, a job, a city. You carry the posture of it forward. Not in the obvious sense of direct memory — in the sense that certain responses, certain ways of reading a room, certain anticipatory flinches were installed during that time and have not been uninstalled. The past bleeds into the present through the body, which has no delete function, only overwrite and habit formation. The overwrite is slow. The new pattern forms on top of the old one, and the old one is still there underneath, printing faintly through.

color into color

what happens at the edge

Hard edges in color exist only in certain materials and certain processes. In watercolor, the wet paint finds wet paint and migrates. In dye, the fibers carry pigment past the pattern boundary. In the eye, colors influence each other's appearance based on proximity — what you call orange next to blue is not what you call orange alone. The perception of color bleeds across the edge of the thing. The edge is a negotiation, not a line.

sound beyond its room

the neighbor's music

Sound does not stop at walls; it transmits through them at a rate determined by the material's density and the frequency. Low frequencies travel furthest. You hear the bass line through the floor. The melody is already gone. What bleeds through is always the foundation — the part that would be easy to ignore if it weren't everywhere, a presence without detail, a rhythm below the threshold of attention that organizes everything without being acknowledged. You only notice it when it stops.

conversation into silence

what continues after it ends

The conversation ends. The sound stops. The conversation does not stop. It continues in the minds of both participants simultaneously, each version now slightly different from the other, each adding the detail and inflection the other person didn't know was there. Both versions are real. Neither is the conversation. The conversation was the overlap between them, which existed only while it was happening and then split into two separate things, each carrying the bleed of the other, diverging.

The bleed is not the failure of the container. It is evidence that the container was real — that something was in it, and that something always exceeds the shape that held it.
