image

JPEG and the theory of what you cannot notice

JPEG compression decomposes an image into overlapping 8×8-pixel blocks and discards the high-frequency components — the fine spatial detail — that human vision is least sensitive to. The algorithm doesn't decide what matters aesthetically; it implements a theory of human perception formalized in 1992. What it calls insignificant is whatever the researchers determined you wouldn't notice. The compression artifact — the blocky halos around edges at high compression — is a record of where that theory was wrong.

Each time you save a JPEG, the quantization step is applied again. The image degrades not in proportion to the number of saves but by repeated application of the same lossy transformation. After enough cycles, what remains is not the original content with some detail missing. It is a new object: the output of repeated approximation, related to the original the way a photocopy of a photocopy is related to the first print. The signal is still technically present. The original is gone.

sound

the telephone voice

Telephone audio is bandlimited to roughly 300–3400Hz — a 3.1kHz window cut from the 20Hz–20kHz audible range. The fundamental frequencies of most speaking voices fall below 300Hz, so the telephone cuts the bass. The fricatives — s, f, th — live above 3400Hz, so the telephone softens the consonants. The voice that arrives is not the voice that left. You receive a narrow-band approximation that preserves enough to identify the speaker and understand the words, and almost nothing else.

That this works as well as it does is its own result: intelligibility is more robust than fidelity. The signal can be drastically reduced and most of the information survives, because language is built for noisy channels. Redundancy is everywhere in speech — consonant clusters, prosodic cues, syntactic expectation — so a 3.1kHz cut takes very little from the meaning and everything from the music of a voice. You can tell who it is. You cannot hear how they're doing.

information

lossy versus lossless

Lossless compression (ZIP, PNG, FLAC) preserves every bit of the original; decompression recovers the file exactly. Lossy compression (JPEG, MP3, HEVC) discards information deemed redundant or imperceptible; the recovered file is a convincing approximation. These represent two theories of what the future needs from the present. Lossless: keep everything, because you don't know what will matter. Lossy: the receiver can reconstruct the rest; send the gist, not the transcript.

Most lossy codecs exploit specific properties of human perception — the eye's insensitivity to high spatial frequencies, the ear's masking of quiet sounds near loud ones. They compress not the signal but the signal as experienced. What falls below the threshold of perception is discarded. The algorithm's theory of perception is therefore embedded in every compressed file, invisibly, shaping what survives. If the theory is wrong about what you can hear, you will hear the wrongness. If correct, the absence is undetectable.

memory

the episodic to the semantic

Episodic memory — the vivid, specific recollection of events — is replaced over time by semantic memory: the gist, the lesson, the category. You remember that the dinner was significant but not what was said. The particular details dissolve; the emotional outline remains. The specific image fades while the interpretation strengthens. This is not a failure of memory but its operating procedure: compress the specific into the general, the event into the type, the conversation into its meaning.

This compression is lossy in a precise sense: the reconstruction is not the original. When you remember a conversation that happened five years ago, you are not playing back a recording. You are reconstructing, from the semantic residue, something that feels like an episode. The constructed memory carries your current understanding of the relationship, the context, the significance — all of which have changed. Memory is not storage. It is retrieval plus reconstruction, in which the reconstruction is always partially a new creation.

form

the haiku

Bashō's most famous poem is seventeen syllables in Japanese. The standard translation takes eight words: old pond / a frog jumps in / sound of water. This is not a description. It is a compression: the whole moment — the silence, the pond, the interruption, the return to silence — rendered in the minimum number of syllables required to transmit the experience. The poem works not in spite of what is absent but because of it. The compression is not a loss. The compression is the work.

What haiku compresses is not information but attention: it takes a moment and reduces it to the exact perceptual elements that carry the most weight, then strips away the scaffolding of explanation. The ratio between what is present and what is implied is extreme. You supply the rest — the mood, the season's meaning, the poet's stillness — from the minimum the poem gives you. The form bets that what you bring is more valuable than what could be added. Usually it is right.
